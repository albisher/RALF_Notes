### SUMMARY
A comprehensive reference for various celestial coordinate systems used in astronomy and astrophysics.

### DETAILS
This file outlines multiple coordinate systems essential for describing positions, motions, and measurements in astronomy. Each system (e.g., Galactic, Equatorial, Heliocentric) serves distinct purposes in tracking celestial objects, their velocities, and spatial orientations relative to different reference frames. These systems are foundational for astrometry, kinematics, and observational astronomy, enabling precise localization and dynamic analysis of stars, galaxies, and other cosmic phenomena.

### KEY_FUNCTIONS
- **Galactic Coordinates**: Defines positions in the Milky Way galaxy, using the galactic center as the origin.
- **Equatorial Coordinates**: Uses celestial equator and right ascension/declination for equatorial observations.
- **Ecliptic Coordinates**: Aligns with Earth’s orbital plane, useful for solar system dynamics.
- **Horizontal Coordinates**: Local observer-centric system (altitude/azimuth), common in ground-based telescopes.
- **Celestial Coordinates**: Generalized framework for celestial mechanics, including spherical coordinates.
- **Heliocentric System**: Earth-centered frame relative to the Sun, critical for planetary motion studies.
- **Barycentric System**: Sun-centered frame accounting for gravitational influences of planets.
- **Local Group Frame**: Aligns with the Local Group galaxy cluster for large-scale galactic studies.
- **Cosmic Microwave Background Frame**: Defines the universe’s early expansion reference.
- **Proper Motion Coordinates**: Tracks stellar movement over time, accounting for parallax.
- **Radial Velocity System**: Measures object speed toward/away from Earth via Doppler shifts.
- **Parallax Measurements**: Determines distance via angular displacement from Earth’s orbit.
- **Photometric Coordinates**: Relates brightness measurements to celestial coordinates.
- **Spectroscopic Position**: Uses Doppler shifts in spectral lines for velocity and composition analysis.
- **Interferometric Frame**: Combines signals from multiple telescopes for high-resolution imaging.
- **Gravitational Coordinates**: Adapts to general relativity for relativistic astrophysics.
- **Relativistic Frame**: Accounts for spacetime curvature in high-energy astrophysical contexts.
