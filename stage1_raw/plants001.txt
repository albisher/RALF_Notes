### SUMMARY
Explores fundamental plant biology, adaptations, and classification, emphasizing photosynthesis, environmental influences, and structural features.

### DETAILS
This document outlines the core biological characteristics of plants, including their ability to perform photosynthesis, essential structural components (roots, stems, leaves, flowers, fruits), and autotrophic nature. It details how environmental factors like light, water, temperature, and soil conditions shape plant morphology and pigmentation, leading to diverse adaptations across biomes. The text also categorizes plants based on vascular tissue presence, reproductive structures (flowers vs. cones), seed types, and morphological traits, supported by a classification summary table.

### KEY_FUNCTIONS
- **Photosynthesis**: Process of converting sunlight, CO₂, and water into sugars and oxygen.
- **Root Adaptations**: Anchorage and nutrient/water absorption in soil.
- **Stem Transport**: Movement of water, nutrients, and food between roots and leaves.
- **Leaf Function**: Photosynthesis, gas exchange, and transpiration regulation.
- **Reproductive Structures**: Flowering (angiosperms) vs. non-flowering (gymnosperms, ferns, mosses).
- **Seed Dispersal**: Enclosed (angiosperms) vs. exposed (gymnosperms) seed development.
- **Environmental Adaptations**: Morphological and pigment changes (e.g., anthocyanins) for survival in biomes.

### CALLOUTS
>[!INFO]- **Key Adaptation Example
> Desert plants like cacti store water in thickened stems and minimize stomata to reduce evaporation, illustrating water conservation adaptations.
>[!WARNING]- **Moss Classification Note
> Bryophytes (mosses) lack vascular tissue, relying solely on diffusion for water uptake—a critical distinction from vascular plants.
